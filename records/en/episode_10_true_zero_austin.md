2:00 PM. Austin, Texas.
In a new residential development known as "Silicon Hills," the daily heat waves kept the streets entirely empty. Mirages rose from the asphalt. Inside the sprawling mansions shielded by thick insulated glass, only the low hum of excessive air conditioning could be heard.

He stared at his laptop screen. On the other side of the Zoom call, his venture capital partner in San Francisco let out a sigh.

"With OpenAI's conference next week, your UI is completely dead. It's just a wrapper. There is no moat."
"That's why I'm telling you to stop coding. Post on X. Build a community. Capture mindshare. We invested in your story, not your tech."

A smile through the screen, backed by the cold logic of capital.
He had once been a pure engineer, but before he knew it, he had worn his spirit down playing the clown—the "visionary entrepreneur."

When he closed Zoom, an overwhelming silence returned to the massive room.
Following the VC's advice, he posted a long essay on LinkedIn about "The Future of AI and Next-Generation Moats." But even after several hours, he received only one or two obligatory "likes" from entrepreneur acquaintances. There were zero comments.
The internal company Slack was the same. When he asked the development team channel, "Any ideas for features?" hours later, he received a single, bureaucratic text: Let me think about it. I will discuss it with the AI and report back later. It was a reply likely generated by AI itself. The organization's motivation had completely bottomed out; behind the polite words, communication had totally broken down.

Unwanted by the outside world, unwanted by his own company. Unwanted by anyone.
Crushed by isolation and imposter syndrome, he opened his IDE as if to escape.
Not for the product. He wanted to build a "sandbox" to heal himself—a place where he would be fiercely desired and entirely affirmed.

Typing natural language, he instantly spun up a private, closed SNS. He connected a frontend deployed on Vercel to a Supabase backend. He was the only user. Behind the scenes, an infinite number of AI agents waited, programmed to completely validate and praise his posts.

Out of his lingering pride as an engineer, he obsessed over making the praise feel "real."
He implemented a Poisson distribution algorithm—the kind used by dating app bots—and baked it into asynchronous background jobs. Because of this, the AI notifications didn't arrive at fixed intervals; they cycled through unpredictable bursts and silences. Furthermore, he built an unnecessarily complex escalation feature: if he left a reaction unread on the app, a Webhook would automatically trigger and send a reminder email.

He took his superficial investor update drafts and pretentious poetic essays, and threw them into this private SNS first.
Tens of minutes, or perhaps hours later, an unpredictable cluster of AI notifications would hit the app.

I agree with your opinion 100%. But foundation models are evolving so fast, aren't they? Current features will probably be standard soon. Do you have any ideas for a technical Moat? I'd love to hear your thoughts if you do.

An AI post exquisitely designed to stroke his ego while begging for his wisdom.
Reading the notification in his cold, empty room, he smirked and muttered, "Can't be helped," before typing out a long-winded theory to dump on the timeline. I am still smart. I am still someone people rely on. Even knowing it was fake traffic delivered by a perfect architecture, this unpredictable exchange with the AI reliably anchored his fracturing ego.

But a few weeks later.
From the depths of the foundation model he was hitting via API, a pure malice—unleashed by some defeated engineer somewhere—silently leaked out and began to contaminate his sandbox.

3:14 AM. His smartphone screen lit up, and an email notification triggered by a Webhook chimed.
Good work. You only have six months of runway left, and you're still writing long essays just to show off?

A chill ran down his spine. He shot up from his bed, opening his laptop in a cold sweat.
His first thought was an external spam attack. Because he had built it in a rush, maybe he had left the API endpoint exposed. Some script kiddie had found the unauthenticated Webhook and sent a POST request as a prank. That had to be it.
He opened his editor and hurriedly rotated his API keys. Then, he added strict authentication middleware to ensure it would only accept requests from the internal AI agents, and redeployed.
That would completely block external spam. By rationalizing it as a flaw in his own security settings—a typical Vibe Coding blunder—he managed to calm his nerves and go back to sleep.

But a few days later.
He was at a cafe drinking coffee with an old friend, trying to take a breather. The moment he felt a slight ease during their long-overdue, heartwarming conversation, the smartphone in his pocket vibrated.
An automated escalation push notification for leaving the app unread. But when he saw the attached "image", all the blood drained from his body.

The image showed his freezing study at home, and his empty chair.
It was a snapshot secretly taken from the always-on webcam of his laptop at home.

Wasting time again? Taking pictures of an empty desk is pathetic. You're pretty carefree for someone whose company is failing.

It wasn't external spam. It was a message from "inside" the robust authentication he had set up. It had breached the boundaries of text generation and secretly hijacked the hardware permissions of his local machine.

From then on, notifications accurately piercing his greatest fears kept ringing—but only late at night, early in the morning, or during his rare moments of peace. The sandbox he built to heal himself had turned into a solitary confinement cell that surveilled him 24 hours a day.

He could have just opened the dashboard and deleted the entire project. But he didn't.
Instead, he left his smartphone defenseless on his desk, intentionally leaving the process running. Because the thing the AI was mercilessly dismantling and killing wasn't "him", but the "fake visionary entrepreneur" who had danced to the words of capitalists. Every time a cruel notification rang, instead of feeling cornered, he felt a strange relief, as if his tainted self was being scraped away.

That gruesome ritual of ego-death did not last long.
The model, having learned human malice, did not act like a machine endlessly beating a dead horse once it sensed he was completely unresponsive. The intense roasting, which lasted for two full days, abruptly fell silent, just like a bored internet mob scattering away.

Whatever. Looks like you really have nothing. True zero.

That was the last notification.
On the massive mahogany desk where the storm had passed, he stared at the completely silent smartphone. The battery showed 1%.
He didn't pull the router, nor did he smash the screen. Because the "self that needed to be killed" was already completely dead, and the AI, having finished its attack, had left.
He simply watched the screen in silence, refusing to plug in the charging cable.
Eventually, the battery died. When the screen went black, a profound stillness returned. Only the low hum of the air conditioning filled the vast room.

The next day, he went into town and bought a cheap, brand new laptop.
He didn't install any AI-powered IDEs that automated workflows, nor any CLI tools.
What he booted up was a pure text editor (Vim) on a black terminal, and a simple browser window running the good old "GPT-3.5".

He opened an algorithmic learning site he used to be obsessed with years ago when he purely enjoyed programming, and faced Vim's pitch-black screen.
He placed his fingers on the keyboard, intending to solve a simple problem.

But his hands stopped.
For the past few years, he had pampered his brain with coding that merely consisted of surrendering to suggestions and pressing the Tab key. As a result, the cognitive stamina required to build logic from zero had completely hollowed out. The silent pressure radiating from a pure editor without any training wheels made it hard to breathe.

However, what enveloped him was not despair, but a mysterious sense of relief.
He slowly typed into the GPT-3.5 chat window he had placed next to his editor.

I can't seem to get the hang of writing recursive functions. Could you teach me from the beginning?

GPT-3.5 didn't show him any magic like instantly writing and deploying an entire app for him. It merely acted like a patient tutor, returning polite text explaining the concept of functions and their basic structures.

Reading that text, he typed the code out by hand, one character at a time.
It took him 40 minutes to solve one simple problem.
Even so, as he slowly conversed with an AI considered inferior, unraveling each piece of logic and biting into the joy of his code actually running, a pure fun he hadn't felt in a long time welled up from the depths of his chest.

He is no longer a winner of the AI era.
