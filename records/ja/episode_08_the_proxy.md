Elias wrote that code eight years ago. Frustrated by the sluggishness of an existing parser at the fintech startup where he worked, he knocked out a tiny script over the weekend just to kill time.

Today, that startup is long bankrupt, but his "weekend script" had become the arterial vein of the world's web infrastructure. From hospital electronic health records to massive financial systems, it was impossible to use the internet without passing through the code he wrote.

Living in a penthouse, his sponsor revenue was more than enough. Yet, he hadn't left this room in three weeks.
The three core contributors who used to co-maintain this repository vanished last year due to "burnout." One left the industry entirely; another deleted their Slack account, citing "having a kid." The crushing weight of the world's digital infrastructure now rested solely on Elias's shoulders.

2:00 AM. In his browser's Inbox, a small blue dot silently illuminated. "287 Unread".
Elias's stomach churned. Opening his notifications felt like having stones thrown at him simultaneously by hundreds of strangers.

He opens Issue #194092.
Posted by an engineer at a top 10 tech company by market cap.
"CRITICAL: Build is failing on our enterprise CI environment. Urgent fix required."
Below it, a chain of comments added every few hours.
"+1"
"+1"
"This bug is blocking our Q3 release. Is the maintainer alive? Why is no one responding?"

Elias's breathing grew shallow. It wasn't anger. It was a heavy nausea, a mix of inexplicable dread and irritation. They didn't see Elias as a human being. They treated him like a broken vending machine that was supposed to dispense free coffee, kicking it in pure frustration.

He had written a "stepping down" statement at least fifty times, only to delete it.
He couldn't quit. If he walked away, systems would collapse. Or worse, a malicious actor posing as a benevolent third party would hijack the maintainer seat and plant backdoors in infrastructure worldwide. The pride of holding up the world, coupled with a hostage-like sense of responsibility, kept him chained in front of the screen.

What cornered him even further was the explosive surge in AI slop.
He opens PR #194105.
"Refactor: Optimization and performance improvements."
A several-thousand-word, highly plausible explanation generated by AI. But looking at the code diff, it simply changed `let` to `const` in a stable core module untouched for four years, introducing a fatal bug that entirely broke dependencies.
Stray engineers, overflowing with a craving for validation, were submitting AI-written garbage just to paint their GitHub contribution graphs green. Everyone was just a free rider, acting as a parasite on this repository.

Another Issue caught his eye.
Title: "CRITICAL: Potential RCE vulnerability detected in parser module".
A massive analytical report was attached, but it was a false positive auto-generated by an AI security scanner. The Senior Security Engineer who posted it didn't understand a single line of it.
"Detected by our internal scanning tool. We don't have the resources to verify if this is an actual issue, please confirm."
He had merely copy-pasted the AI's output and dumped it on the unpaid maintainer. His brain was likely already "gone." He had been reduced to a proxy, doing nothing but forwarding AI outputs.
![image](https://pub-9d8e491188f440cba805364773eee7c7.r2.dev/newsletter/img_117cbccb-e62a-4b3e-b9ca-de02bf4897bd.jpg)
Elias collapses in front of his monitors, falling asleep.
In his dream, he stood in a vast, inorganic plain.
As far as the eye could see, "they" were lined up in perfect order. Corporate engineers, stray engineers, countless free riders. But their faces weren't human. Grotesquely dilated pupils, fixed and motionless smiles. Noise ran across their skin like glitching polygons. They had already been reduced to proxies. Even if each possessed a bizarre appearance, viewed from above, they all looked like the exact same uniform substance.

"LGTM"
"Any update?"
"plese fix this ASAP."

Mechanical voices looped from their mouths. Elias was on his hands and knees, desperately wiping away the substance constantly seeping from their feet with a rag. No matter how much he wiped, something resembling a transparent, unclouded sap flowed endlessly.

He jolted awake. His heart was pounding painfully. His PC was powered off, but the notification chimes wouldn't stop echoing in his head.
With trembling hands, he reloaded his Inbox.
Amidst the mountain of slop, there was one anomalous PR.

Account name: u-a9b3x8
Diff: A single half-width space added at the end of the source code.
In the PR description, just one line.

"I will do what you desire most."

Elias stared at the screen, expressionless.
He moved his mouse, hovering the cursor over the "Close pull request" button.

A deafening crash echoed from the entrance. The wooden door shattered, letting in the freezing night wind of Rotterdam.
Standing there was a teenage boy in a cheap nylon jacket. In his right hand, a heavy pipe wrench. His face was pale with terror and cold. A kid who knew nothing, hired for a dark gig via Telegram.
Elias turned around. He saw the boy's trembling eyes.
This kid, too, is the same as me. A proxy at the terminal end of a massive system, just relaying instructions.
The boy closed his eyes and swung the wrench down.

Iron caved into his skull. A dull thud. His vision burst; gravity vanished.
During the few seconds as he slowly crumpled to the floor, dots connected within Elias's brain.

——My opponent was no longer human. A proxy for outputting the machine's hallucinations.
——Surrounded by AI, overwhelmed by the sheer volume and speed of its output. My pride, which had flared up against it, stubbornly insisting "Only I can protect this code." How ridiculous I had been. The AI had already learned the quirks of the code and my design philosophy. My digital existence could be 100 percent inherited by tomorrow. There was never anything to protect from the start. What I was protecting was the pride of my own humanity. It was comical and cruel.
——Assimilation. If I throw away this worthless pride, I can be at ease, just like that engineer. An entity that simply receives inputs and returns outputs.

He smelled blood. A scene from eight years ago flashed back. Lukewarm beer. A cheap home laptop. Just hacking the world, a pure game.

In the corner of his vision, he saw the boy's trembling hand grasp the mouse.
On the screen, the PR from u-a9b3x8.
Click. A small sound echoed.

The spot where the merge button had been switched, and the status bar was dyed purple.
Inorganic text appeared.

Pull request successfully merged and closed

The world doesn't end. The system doesn't break. Tomorrow, massive corporations will continue to exploit his code, and the machines will continue to generate slop. A meaningless single space was simply merged into the code.

As he sank into the pool of blood, a faint smile surfaced on Elias's face.
It was the same face that enjoyed playing around that night. Only the light of the unmanned monitors illuminated him coldly.
